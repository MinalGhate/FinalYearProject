Frequently Asked Questions

What information is included in an SSL/TLS certificate? + -

An SSL/TLS certificate contains details such as domain name, certificate authority, certificate authority's digital signature, issuance date, and expiration date.

Why is the validity period of an SSL/TLS certificate important? + -

The validity period ensures that outdated security algorithms are replaced promptly, maintaining optimal security and reducing risks associated with expired certificates.

What does an expired SSL/TLS certificate mean? + -

An expired certificate cannot secure a connection, potentially exposing data to risks. Websites with expired certificates may be marked as "Not Secure".

How can I check how many days are left until my certificate expires? + -

Certificates are marked as secure (green) if more than 90 days remain, as caution (yellow) between 30-90 days, and "Not Secure" (red) if less than 30 days or expired.

What should I do if my SSL/TLS certificate is expiring soon or has expired? + -

If expiring soon, renew the certificate to avoid security risks. If expired, renew it immediately to prevent breaches.

How are SSL/TLS certificates graded in terms of security? + -

Grades range from A (best security) to F (critical issues), based on algorithms, key sizes, hash functions, and vulnerabilities.

What are the best practices for secure SSL/TLS configuration? + -

Use TLS v1.2 or higher, RSA with 2048-bit keys, avoid SHA-1, and enable Perfect Forward Secrecy (PFS) for additional security.

What is the significance of the Common Name (CN) field in an SSL/TLS certificate? + -

The Common Name (CN) identifies the entity the certificate secures. It's used for trust verification, although modern browsers prioritize the Subject Alternative Name (SAN) field.
